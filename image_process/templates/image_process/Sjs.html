What is SJS-TEN?

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) and Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN) are severe skin reactions primarily caused by medications. They represent a spectrum of disease, with SJS being the less severe form and TEN the more severe. Both conditions involve extensive damage to the skin and mucous membranes, leading to significant morbidity and potential mortality.

Types of SJS-TEN

1. Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)

Definition: SJS is characterized by a skin detachment affecting less than 10% of the body surface area. It typically begins with flu-like symptoms followed by a painful rash that can lead to blistering and skin loss.

Symptoms: Initial symptoms include fever, malaise, and upper respiratory symptoms. This is followed by the appearance of erythematous, targetoid lesions that progress to flaccid bullae and erosions. Mucosal involvement is common, affecting areas such as the mouth, eyes, and genitalia15.

Complications: The condition can lead to severe complications such as infections, sepsis, and multi-organ failure. The mortality rate for SJS is around 10%47.

2. Overlap Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis

Definition: This category applies when 10% to 30% of the body surface area is affected. It represents an intermediate severity between SJS and TEN.

Symptoms: Symptoms combine features of both SJS and TEN, with significant skin loss and mucosal involvement but not as extensive as in TEN.

Complications: Similar risks as SJS but with increased severity due to greater skin involvement.

3. Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)

Definition: TEN is a life-threatening condition where more than 30% of the body surface area is affected by skin detachment. It is often considered a progression from SJS.

Symptoms: Patients experience widespread blistering and peeling of the skin, including extensive mucosal damage. The initial flu-like symptoms are followed by rapid deterioration into large areas of denuded skin23.

Complications: The risk of complications is significantly higher in TEN, with mortality rates ranging from 25% to 50%. Complications can include severe dehydration, pneumonia, sepsis, organ failure, and death46.

When to Consult?

Consultation with a healthcare provider should be sought in the following scenarios:

Initial Symptoms: If an individual experiences flu-like symptoms followed by a painful rash after starting a new medication.

Skin Changes: Rapid development of blistering or peeling skin, especially if it involves mucous membranes.

Worsening Condition: If there are signs of systemic illness such as fever, malaise, or significant pain.

Mucosal Involvement: Any signs of mouth ulcers or conjunctivitis should prompt immediate medical attention.

Severe Reactions: Patients with known drug allergies or previous episodes of SJS/TEN should be closely monitored when starting new medications.

Both SJS and TEN are medical emergencies requiring immediate hospitalization for supportive care and management of complications. Early recognition and intervention are crucial for improving outcomes in affected individuals157.
