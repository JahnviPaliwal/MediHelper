What is Vitiligo?

Vitiligo is a chronic autoimmune disorder characterized by the loss of skin pigmentation, resulting in white patches on the skin. This occurs when melanocytes, the cells responsible for producing melanin (the pigment that gives skin its color), are destroyed or cease to function properly. The exact cause of vitiligo remains unclear, but it is believed to involve a combination of genetic factors, immune system changes, stress, and environmental triggers such as sun exposure or chemical irritants123.

Types of Vitiligo

Vitiligo is classified into several types based on the pattern and distribution of depigmented patches:

1. Non-Segmental Vitiligo (NSV)

Description: This is the most common form, accounting for about 90% of cases. It typically appears symmetrically on both sides of the body.

Characteristics: Patches can develop on any part of the body, including the face, hands, arms, and feet. The patches may gradually enlarge over time.

Subtypes:

Generalized Vitiligo: Widespread loss of pigmentation across various body parts.

Acrofacial Vitiligo: Affects areas such as the face, hands, and feet.

Mucosal Vitiligo: Involves mucous membranes, including inside the mouth and nose.

2. Segmental Vitiligo (SV)

Description: Less common than NSV, segmental vitiligo affects only one side or segment of the body.

Characteristics: Typically begins at a younger age (often in childhood) and progresses for a limited time (usually 6 to 12 months) before stabilizing. The patches in this type are usually more stable and do not spread as much as in NSV.

3. Universal Vitiligo

Description: A rare form where depigmentation occurs over most of the body.

Characteristics: This type can lead to a near-total loss of skin color and is often associated with significant psychological distress due to its extensive appearance.

4. Localized Vitiligo

Description: Involves a small number of patches that are confined to specific areas.

Characteristics: These patches may remain stable for years without further spreading.

When to Consult a Dermatologist?

Consultation with a dermatologist is advised in several situations:

Initial Diagnosis: If you notice unexplained white patches on your skin that are spreading or changing in appearance.

Psychological Impact: If the condition affects your self-esteem or mental health due to cosmetic concerns.

Management Options: To discuss treatment options such as topical medications, phototherapy, or surgical interventions aimed at restoring skin color or managing symptoms.

Associated Symptoms: If you experience other symptoms like itching, inflammation in other areas (such as eyes), or if you have a family history of autoimmune diseases that could be related.
